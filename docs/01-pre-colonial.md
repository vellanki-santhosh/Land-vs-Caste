# The Pre-Colonial Foundation: Varna, Jati, and the Agrarian Hierarchy

## Summary

Long before the East India Company arrived, India's land was not owned in the modern sense — it was controlled through a web of ritual obligation, royal grant, and communal custom that systematically privileged upper-caste households. Understanding this baseline is essential to understanding why later colonial and post-colonial interventions failed to fundamentally alter the distribution of land.

---

## The Varna Framework and Land

The ancient varna system divided society into four broad categories — Brahmin (priests and scholars), Kshatriya (warriors and rulers), Vaishya (traders and cultivators), and Shudra (service and artisanal labour) — plus communities entirely outside the system, the *avarnas*, whom later discourse would call "untouchables" and the Constitution would designate Scheduled Castes.

In this scheme, the **king** (generally Kshatriya) held ultimate sovereignty over land, while **Brahmins** received *agrahara* (tax-free land grants) in perpetuity for ritual and administrative services. The actual cultivating strata ranged from prosperous peasant-owners of middle castes to near-landless Shudra tenants and avarna labourers who worked fields they could never own.

Key texts encoding these rights include:
- **Manusmriti** (c. 200 BCE – 200 CE): codified Brahmin rights to gifts of land; prohibited Shudras from accumulating property that might rival their social superiors.
- **Arthashastra** (attributed to Kautilya, c. 300 BCE): detailed royal land revenue systems but reserved administrative roles for upper castes.
- **Regional dharmashastra traditions**: varied by region but consistently placed productive land in upper-caste or royal hands.

## Jati and Local Agrarian Order

Abstract varna is less important on the ground than the thousands of localised *jati* (sub-caste) communities, each occupying a specific niche in the agrarian economy:

| Role | Typical Jati Category | Land Relation |
|------|----------------------|---------------|
| Revenue farmer / village headman | Upper peasant castes (Patidar, Reddy, Nair, Jat, etc.) | Hereditary cultivating rights; could mortgage/sublet |
| Artisan and service castes | Middle OBC jatis | Customary plots attached to jajmani obligations |
| Agricultural labourer | Dalit jatis (Chamar, Mahar, Mala, Paraiyar, etc.) | No land rights; labour in exchange for grain payments (*vetti*) |
| Forest and shifting cultivator | Adivasi communities | Usufructuary access to commons and forest; no revenue title |

The **jajmani system** — a network of reciprocal obligations between specialist castes — may appear egalitarian on the surface, but it locked each community into a defined economic role enforced by social sanction. Dalit families who attempted to cultivate independently were subject to social boycott, physical violence, and denial of access to village commons.

## Regional Variations

### South India
The Tamil and Telugu deltaic regions developed elaborate irrigation systems controlled by dominant peasant castes (Vellalar, Kamma, Reddy). Dalit Paraiyar and Mala communities were *pannaiyal* — bound agricultural labourers attached to landed households without wage or security of tenure.

### North India
The Indo-Gangetic plain saw more frequent political disruption, with Rajput, Bhumihar, and Brahmin zamindars claiming customary rights over villages. Chamar and Dusadh communities, though numerous, held no cultivating rights and were bound to the *lathait* (lathi-armed landlord) system.

### Central India and the Deccan
Adivasi communities — Gond, Bhil, Oraon, Ho, Santhal — exercised governance over forest and hill territories under their own polities. The precolonial Mughal and Maratha states extracted tribute but rarely displaced internal land arrangements. Colonial mapping and classification would later convert these customary rights into dispossession.

---

## The Mughal Interlude

The Mughal agrarian system, described in detail by Abul Fazl in the *Ain-i-Akbari* (c. 1590s), introduced **mansabdari** and **jagirdari** assignments of revenue rights over territories. These were theoretically temporary grants (not permanent ownership), but in practice became hereditary in the hands of existing landed elites — reinforcing, rather than disrupting, caste-based landownership.

The **zamindars** who collected revenue for the Mughal state were predominantly upper-caste intermediaries. Their position crystallised over the Mughal centuries, setting the stage for the British decision to recognise them as proprietors.

## What Pre-Colonial Land Looked Like for Dalit Communities

The evidence — from inscriptions, revenue records, travellers' accounts, and oral history — is consistent:

- Dalit communities were **confined to the margins** of the village settlement (*cherikalu*, *agraharams*' outskirts, or separate hamlets altogether).
- They worked as agricultural labourers, were paid in grain, and had no claim on the land they cultivated.
- They were barred from owning draught animals in many regions, which alone prevented independent cultivation.
- Village commons — grazing land, water bodies, forest — were administered by the dominant-caste *panchayat* and effectively closed to Dalit use.

This was the structural baseline that colonialism encountered, codified, and in many ways intensified.
