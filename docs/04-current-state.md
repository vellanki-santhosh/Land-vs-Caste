# The Current State: Who Owns India's Land Today

## Summary

Seventy-five years after Independence, national surveys consistently reveal a 3–4× gap in agricultural landholding between scheduled-caste households and forward-caste households, with Adivasi land rights remaining heavily qualified and contested. This chapter compiles key datasets and identifies the principal mechanisms through which inequality in land ownership is reproduced in the present.

---

## Key Data Points

### Agricultural Landholding

**NSSO 77th Round (2018–19) — Agricultural Households Survey:**

| Social Group | Average Operated Area (hectares) | % Households Owning Land |
|---|---|---|
| Scheduled Castes (SC) | 0.26 | ~40 % |
| Scheduled Tribes (ST) | 0.52 | ~55 % (often untitled) |
| Other Backward Classes (OBC) | 0.61 | ~60 % |
| Upper Castes / Forward Castes | 1.13 | ~78 % |
| **All India** | **0.51** | **~56 %** |

Notes:
- "Owning land" includes plots less than 0.01 hectare and homestead land; purely agricultural ownership rates are lower.
- Operated area figures refer to the kharif season and include owned, leased-in, and otherwise operated land.

### Agricultural Census (2015–16)

- Total operational holdings: 146 million.
- Average holding size: 1.08 hectares (down from 2.28 in 1970–71 due to fragmentation).
- Holdings < 1 hectare (marginal): 86 % of total holdings but control only 47 % of area.
- Holdings > 10 hectares (large): 0.4 % of total holdings but control ~10 % of area (and this likely understates concentration because benami holdings are not aggregated).

### NFHS-5 (2019–21) — Asset Ownership

NFHS measures **individual land ownership** (distinct from household operational land):

| Indicator | SC Women | ST Women | OBC Women | Upper-Caste Women |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sole/joint owner of agricultural land | 10.5 % | 16.8 % | 14.0 % | 21.3 % |
| Sole/joint owner of a house | 42.3 % | 50.4 % | 47.2 % | 56.8 % |

Gender intersects with caste to produce the deepest dispossession: Dalit women are the least likely to hold any land title.

## The Land Gini Coefficient

The Gini coefficient for **land distribution** (0 = perfect equality, 1 = one household owns everything):

| Country / Year | Land Gini |
|---|---|
| India, 2015–16 | ~0.75 |
| India, 1960–61 | ~0.65 |
| China, 2015 | ~0.50 |
| Brazil, 2017 | ~0.72 |
| Bangladesh, 2019 | ~0.50 |

India's land inequality has **increased** since Independence, not decreased.

## Regional Patterns

### Bihar and Eastern UP
Historically the heartland of zamindari, Bihar shows some of the sharpest caste–land divides:
- Musahar, Chamar, and Dusadh communities — collectively among the most numerous Dalit groups in the state — overwhelmingly landless.
- Land is effectively controlled by Bhumihar, Rajput, and Brahmin households in much of rural Bihar, though Yadav and Kurmi communities have made gains through tenancy in some areas.
- National Crime Records Bureau data show Bihar and UP consistently among states with the highest SC/ST atrocity cases, most related to land and labour disputes.

### Telangana and Andhra Pradesh
- Post-bifurcation both states have large numbers of Dalit agricultural labourers (Mala and Madiga communities) still working under informal share-cropping arrangements with dominant-caste Kamma, Reddy, and Velama landlords.
- Telangana's Dalit Bandhu scheme (launched 2021) allocated ₹10 lakh per beneficiary Dalit household — a direct investment model, not a land redistribution model — recognising that land redistribution via reform machinery had been exhausted.

### Punjab and Haryana
- Post-Green Revolution prosperity is concentrated among Jat Sikh and Jat Hindu proprietors.
- Dalit (Mazhabi Sikh, Ramdasia, Valmiki) households constitute ~32 % of Punjab's population but own only ~3 % of agricultural land.
- Shamlat (common/village) land, which was historically a supplementary resource for Dalit communities, has been encroached upon and registered in the names of upper-caste village panchayats.

### Gujarat
- Adivasi (Bhil, Chaudhary, Rathwa) communities in South and East Gujarat face heavy land alienation pressure from industrial projects (SEZs, mining).
- Despite the Gujarat Scheduled Tribes (Restriction on Transfer of Immovable Property) Act 1991, large-scale irregular transfers continue; restoration proceedings are rare.
- Patel-community dominance in irrigated agriculture contrasts with Adivasi and Dalit dependence on rainfed or wasteland plots.

### Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha
- These states have the highest concentration of Adivasi populations and the most intense conflict between forest rights and extractive industry.
- Coal, iron ore, bauxite, and infrastructure projects have displaced an estimated 2–3 million Adivasi people since 1950 (People's Union for Democratic Rights estimates).
- Land Conflict Watch documents over 200 active land conflicts in these three states alone, most involving Adivasi communities vs. state and corporate interests.

## Mechanisms of Ongoing Dispossession

### 1. Informal Tenancy without Legal Protection
Because formal tenancy is technically illegal or heavily regulated in most states, actual lease arrangements go unrecorded. Dalit and small-OBC farmers who lease-in land have no written contract, no security of tenure, and no access to crop insurance or institutional credit on the leased plot.

### 2. Debt and Distress Sale
- Average agricultural debt of SC households: ₹47,000 (NSSO 77th Round) vs. ₹1,04,000 for all agricultural households — but SC households have far less land as collateral, so the debt-to-asset ratio is far higher.
- Distress sales during drought years, crop failure, or medical emergency are not tracked, but case studies across states document their scale.

### 3. Land Acquisition for Development
The Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act 2013 (LARR) was meant to ensure consent and better compensation. Amendments and state-level modifications have weakened consent requirements for "industrial corridor" and affordable housing projects.

Adivasi and Dalit communities — whose land is cheapest and whose political voice is weakest — disproportionately lose land to such projects. Compensation is paid in cash and does not restore livelihood, community cohesion, or cultural attachment to place.

### 4. Failure to Update Records
Digital Land Records Management Programme (DILRMP) has computerised ~96 % of Records of Rights across states. But computerisation reflects historical inequality; it does not correct unregistered rights, disputed boundaries, or ceiling-surplus land sitting undistributed on state books.

### 5. Homestead Land
An estimated 10–15 million Dalit rural households lack secure title even to the house-plot (*vastu sthala*, *griha bhoomi*) on which they live. Without homestead title, families cannot:
- Obtain home loans.
- Access certain government schemes tied to land records.
- Resist eviction during road widening, dam construction, etc.

## Land Conflict Data

[Land Conflict Watch](https://www.landconflictwatch.org/) maintains a publicly accessible database of land conflicts in India.

Selected findings (as of 2023):
- **Active conflicts tracked:** 780+
- **Area under conflict:** 3.6 million hectares (≈ 2 % of India's total land area)
- **People affected:** 8.9 million
- **Share of conflicts involving SC/ST communities:** ~68 %
- **Primary conflict types:** industrial/infrastructure (42 %), forest / conservation (25 %), agriculture (18 %)

---

## Conclusion

Contemporary data paint a picture of structural continuity. The pre-colonial and colonial pattern — in which productive land accrues to upper and dominant castes while Dalit and Adivasi communities remain landless or near-landless — has persisted through decades of formal reform. Urbanisation and industrialisation have added new vectors of dispossession rather than replacing old ones. For the majority of India's 180 million Dalit rural inhabitants, land remains an aspiration rather than a possession.
