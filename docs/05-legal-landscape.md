# The Legal Landscape: Laws, Judgements, and Persisting Gaps

## Summary

Indian law contains substantial provisions meant to protect Dalit and Adivasi land rights and redistribute land more equitably. Yet there is a persistent gap between what the law says and what it delivers on the ground. This chapter surveys the principal legislation, landmark court decisions, and structural gaps that define the present legal situation.

## Constitutional Provisions

| Article | Content Relevant to Land and Caste |
|---------|-------------------------------------|
| Art. 14 | Equality before law |
| Art. 15 | Non-discrimination (with positive duty under 15(4) for SC/ST) |
| Art. 17 | Abolition of untouchability |
| Art. 21 | Right to life — interpreted to include livelihood and shelter |
| Art. 31A | Protection for land reform laws from Art. 19 challenge |
| Art. 31B + 9th Schedule | Land reform acts shielded from fundamental-rights scrutiny |
| Art. 38 | State to minimise inequalities in income and status |
| Art. 39(b)(c) | Directive — ownership of material resources, prevention of concentration |
| Art. 46 | Promote educational and economic interests of SC/ST |
| Art. 243G + Sch. 11, Entry 2 | Panchayat role in land improvement, land reform implementation |
| Arts. 244, 244A + 5th/6th Schedule | Tribal area governance — partial shield from land alienation |

The **Fifth Schedule** (applicable to most tribal areas) requires the Governor's assent for laws transferring tribal land and mandates a Tribes Advisory Council. In practice, many Schedule V notifications have been used to extend development regulations rather than protect rights.

## Landmark Court Decisions

### I.C. Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967)
The Supreme Court held that Parliament could not amend fundamental rights (including the then-existing right to property). This forced constitutional amendments (24th, 25th, 29th Amendments) to protect land reform laws and ultimately led to the 44th Amendment (1978) abolishing the right to property as a fundamental right.

### Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)
The court upheld the power of Parliament to amend the Constitution (overruling Golaknath) but established the **basic structure doctrine**, holding that the Constitution's fundamental features could not be amended. This settled the constitutional basis for land reform legislation.

### Waman Rao v. Union of India (1981)
Upheld the Ninth Schedule's protection for land reform laws, though later clarified in **I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007)** that Ninth Schedule laws could still be struck down if they violated the basic structure.

### Samatha v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1997)
The Supreme Court held that **government/forest land in Schedule V areas could not be leased to non-tribal mining companies**, since tribal land protections applied to government as a lessor. This was a landmark protection for Adivasi land rights — but has been routinely circumvented through creative project structuring.

### Nandini Sundar v. State of Chhattisgarh (2011)
Addressed displacement and violence in Bastar; the court's observations connected land alienation with human rights violations — an unusual acknowledgment of the link in constitutional jurisprudence.

### Orissa Mining Corporation v. Ministry of Environment and Forests (2013)
The Supreme Court required **gram sabha consent** under the Forest Rights Act before mining in the Niyamgiri hills could proceed; the gram sabhas voted against. Established a precedent for FRA-based community consent.

## SC/ST Land Alienation Prevention Laws

Most states with substantial SC/ST populations have enacted laws preventing transfer of SC/ST land to non-SC/ST persons without government permission. Examples:

| State | Law | Key Provision |
|-------|-----|---------------|
| AP / Telangana | AP Scheduled Areas Land Transfer Regulation (1959) | Void transfers from tribal to non-tribal |
| Gujarat | Gujarat Scheduled Tribes (Restriction on Transfer) Act 1991 | Restoration of illegally transferred tribal land |
| Karnataka | Karnataka Scheduled Castes Sub-Allocation and Tribal Sub-Plan Act 1990 | Budget sub-allocation; weak land provision |
| Rajasthan | Rajasthan Colonisation Act 1954, Section 42B | SC/ST allottees cannot alienate within 10 years |
| Maharashtra | Maharashtra Land Revenue Code — Section 36A | Government approval needed for SC/ST transfers |

**Common problems:**
- Courts have interpreted "transfer" narrowly, exempting mortgages, family partitions, and long-term leases.
- Restoration petitions take 10–20 years and succeed in only a minority of cases.
- Gram panchayats and revenue officials who should enforce these laws are often from dominant castes with a conflict of interest.

## Key Legislative Gaps

### 1. No Comprehensive Dalit Land Rights Act
Despite decades of advocacy, India has no national law specifically guaranteeing a minimum land entitlement or homestead right for Dalit households. Various commissions — including the National Commission for Scheduled Castes and successive Planning Commission sub-groups — have recommended such a law. It has never been enacted.

### 2. Informal Tenancy Protection
Tenancy reform laws in most states technically prohibit sub-letting (to prevent landlords from circumventing ceilings through formal tenancy). In practice this means actual tenants — overwhelmingly Dalit and small-OBC families — are not recorded and cannot access crop insurance, input subsidies, or institutional credit. A model Tenancy Act was issued by NITI Aayog in 2016 and adopted by a handful of states, but coverage and enforcement remain limited.

### 3. Forest Rights Act Implementation
The FRA has been in force since 2008 but:
- About 50% of individual claims and 41% of community claims have been rejected without adequate reasoning (National Forum of Forest Peoples and Forest Workers data).
- The Union Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change and State Forest Departments retain the power to de-notify Community Forest Resource areas.
- Joint Pattas (joint land titles including women) have been issued in only a fraction of recognised claims, contrary to the Act's intent.

### 4. Ceiling Surplus Land
An estimated 0.5–1 million hectares of ceiling-surplus land declared by states remains undistributed decades after vesting orders. This land sits in limbo — on paper belonging to the government, in practice occupied by the same families from whom it was declared surplus.

### 5. Urban Land and Homestead
Urban land — including slum land and peri-urban agricultural land — falls mostly outside agricultural reform architecture. Dalit and Adivasi communities who have migrated to cities overwhelmingly live in informal settlements without tenure security. Despite 30 years of "slum rehabilitation" schemes, in-situ titling of slum residents remains rare.

## The Way Forward: Policy Recommendations

Scholarship and advocacy consistently identify the following as high-priority interventions:

1. **Distribute ceiling-surplus land** still in government possession, with priority to landless SC/ST households and Dalit women.
2. **Enact a Homestead Rights Act** guaranteeing a minimum homestead plot to every rural household lacking land.
3. **Record all tenancy agreements** without penalising the tenant — protect tenants against eviction without deregistering the landlord's title.
4. **Strengthen FRA implementation** — require written, reasoned rejections; independent appellate review; and integration with revenue records.
5. **Integrate land rights with social protection** — crop insurance, MGNREGS, PM-KISAN, and housing schemes should be available to landless agricultural labourers and informal tenants, not only title-holders.
6. **Community land rights** — formalise gram sabha authority over common land, shamlat, and panchayat waste; prevent its diversion without community consent.
7. **Gendered land rights** — mandate joint pattas (husband-wife) for all new allotments; prioritise Dalit women in land distribution programmes.
