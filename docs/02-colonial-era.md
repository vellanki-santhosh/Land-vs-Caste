# The Colonial Era: How British Land Systems Crystallised Caste Privilege

## Summary

British colonialism did not invent the link between caste and land in India, but it decisively transformed it. By converting fluid customary claims into fixed, legally defensible property rights — and by awarding those rights overwhelmingly to existing upper-caste intermediaries — British land-revenue systems locked in structural inequality that persists today. Three revenue systems shaped this process: the **Permanent Settlement**, the **ryotwari system**, and the **mahalwari system**.

---

## The Permanent Settlement (1793)

Introduced by Governor-General Cornwallis in Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa, the Permanent Settlement converted the Mughal *zamindar* — previously a revenue-collecting middleman — into a **heritable private proprietor** of land. The settlement fixed the land revenue demand permanently in exchange for the zamindar's secure title.

### Consequences for Caste and Land

1. **Consolidation of upper-caste ownership.** Bengal's zamindars were disproportionately Brahmin and Kayastha. The settlement gave them an alienable title they had not previously held — transforming ritual authority into market property.

2. **Dispossession of cultivating peasants.** Actual cultivators (*raiyats*), who held customary rights of occupancy, now faced zamindars empowered to evict for non-payment. Middle and lower peasant castes lost security of tenure; Dalit labourers lost even the marginal customary protections of the jajmani system.

3. **Credit and debt bondage.** Zamindars borrowed from moneylenders (often Marwari or Brahmin traders) using their new title as collateral. When they defaulted, land passed to urban merchants — but the cultivators' condition did not improve; they merely acquired a new absentee landlord.

4. **Female exclusion.** British property law privileged male-line inheritance, further concentrating land away from women across castes — though Dalit and Adivasi women, who had exercised some usufructuary rights in customary systems, faced the sharpest losses.

---

## The Ryotwari System

In Madras (1820) and Bombay (1820s–1830s), British administrators — influenced by utilitarian reformers — bypassed intermediaries and settled directly with individual cultivators (*raiyats*). In principle this was less feudal. In practice:

- The **revenue demand was set so high** (typically one-third to one-half of gross produce) that cultivators were pushed into chronic debt.
- Land could be sold for arrears, concentrating holdings in the hands of moneylenders and dominant-caste farmers who could absorb risk.
- Dalit labourers remained entirely outside the settlement — they had no *raiyati* rights at all, since they owned no land to settle with.
- The Madras Permanent Settlement later extended zamindari arrangements in many areas, blurring the distinction with Bengal.

---

## The Mahalwari System

In the North-Western Provinces and Punjab, revenue was settled with the *mahal* (village community) collectively, held by the dominant proprietary brotherhood (*biradari*). This recognised existing village power structures — dominated by Jat, Brahmin, and Rajput communities — while excluding lower castes and landless labourers from any proprietary share.

---

## Forest Settlements and Adivasi Dispossession

Parallel to the agricultural revenue settlements, the colonial state asserted **sovereignty over India's forests** through a series of acts:

| Legislation | Year | Key Effect |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| Indian Forest Act | 1865 | First assertion of state ownership over forests |
| Indian Forest Act | 1878 | Created Reserved and Protected Forests; criminalised customary use |
| Indian Forest Act | 1927 | Consolidated colonial forest law; remained in force until 2006 |

Adivasi communities — whose entire economic and cultural life was organised around forest access — were converted overnight from rights-holders to illegal encroachers. The *Forest Settlement Officers* who demarcated Reserved Forests routinely ignored oral evidence of customary rights. Adivasi resistance (the Santhal Rebellion 1855, Munda Ulgulan 1899–1900, and dozens of smaller uprisings) was met with military force.

---

## Debt and Land Alienation

A distinctive mechanism of colonial-era land dispossession was **moneylender debt**:

1. Colonial revenue demand had to be paid in cash, forcing subsistence farmers into markets.
2. Poor harvests led to borrowing from Brahmin, Bania, or Chetty moneylenders at 24–120 % annual interest.
3. Compound interest quickly made the debt unpayable; land was mortgaged and then sold.
4. By the 1870s–90s, land concentration in the hands of moneylending castes and absentee landlords prompted British concern (primarily about political stability, not welfare).
5. **Deccan Agriculturists' Relief Act (1879)**, **Land Alienation Act, Punjab (1900)**, and similar measures tried to protect "agricultural castes" from "non-agricultural castes" — categories that mapped fairly closely onto caste, further institutionalising caste as a legal category in property law.

---

## Census and Caste Classification

The colonial census, introduced systematically in 1872, required every Indian to be assigned a caste. This:
- **Reified fluid jati identities** into fixed administrative categories.
- Created official lists of "Criminal Tribes" (1871 Act) — predominantly Dalit and nomadic communities — stripping them of movement rights and land access.
- Provided the bureaucratic infrastructure for later caste-based land records.

Land records (*khatauni*, *khasra*, *pattas*) systematically recorded caste alongside ownership, creating paper trails that are both a historical record of dispossession and, paradoxically, the evidentiary basis for subsequent reform.

---

## The Scale of Colonial Land Concentration

By the time of Independence:
- **Bengal:** 25 million acres controlled by approximately 170,000 zamindars; an estimated 30 million landless or near-landless labourers.
- **Madras:** ~17 % of agricultural area held by zamindars in inamdari tenures; the majority of Dalit families landless.
- **Punjab:** roughly 6 % of proprietors controlled more than 50 % of land.
- **All-India:** the 1946–47 pre-Independence survey estimated that roughly 15 % of rural households controlled about 60 % of agricultural land.

The structural starting point for independent India's land reform was, in other words, extreme inequality amplified by 150 years of colonial property law.
