# Land vs Caste: Who Owns India's Land — and Who Never Has

> *A structural divide that predates colonialism and outlasts legislation.*

## Overview

India's land question is inseparable from its caste question. For more than two millennia, ritual hierarchy and agrarian ownership have reinforced each other: upper-caste communities accumulated hereditary claims over productive land while Dalit, Adivasi, and most OBC communities were confined to landless or near-landless labour. Colonial land-revenue systems deepened these inequalities, and the land reforms of the 1950s–70s left the fundamental structure intact. Today, despite a constitutional guarantee of equality and decades of legislation, an estimated **60 % of rural Dalit households own no agricultural land at all**.

This repository documents that history and its present-day consequences through primary data, legislative analysis, and regional case studies.

## Key Facts at a Glance

- **Land Gini coefficient (India, 2015–16):** ~0.75 — among the highest in Asia.
- **Scheduled Caste households owning agricultural land:** ~40 % (NFHS-5, 2019–21).
- **Scheduled Tribe households owning agricultural land:** ~54 %, but often forest land without title.
- **Average land holding of a Dalit household:** 0.26 hectares vs 1.13 hectares for a forward-caste household (NSSO 77th Round).
- **Ceiling-surplus land actually distributed:** ~2.5 million hectares out of ~7 million declared surplus (Ministry of Rural Development, 2022).

## Themes

### 1. The Pre-Colonial Foundation
The varna system placed land stewardship and revenue collection in the hands of Brahmin priests and Kshatriya warriors, while Vaishyas managed trade. Shudras — and the communities outside the four-fold system entirely — provided agricultural and manual labour without hereditary land rights. Regional variations existed, but the structural pattern was consistent: productive land as a caste prerogative.

### 2. Colonial Crystallisation
The British Permanent Settlement of 1793 (Bengal), and the later ryotwari and mahalwari systems, codified and monetised existing hierarchies. Zamindars — predominantly upper-caste — received heritable, transferable titles. Landless communities, prevented from accessing courts or credit, sank further into debt bondage. The forest settlements similarly dispossessed Adivasi communities who had practised usufructuary rights for generations.

### 3. The Reform That Wasn't
Between 1950 and 1972 every Indian state passed land reform legislation. Ceiling laws, tenancy reforms, abolition of zamindari — the statute books were full. Yet enforcement was sabotaged at every level: surveys were falsified, benami transfers shielded excess holdings, and the courts — staffed by the same landed elite — stayed implementation. By the 1980s the reform window had effectively closed.

### 4. The Present Impasse
NSSO and NFHS data consistently show a 3–4× gap in per-household landholding between forward castes and Scheduled Castes. Rapid urbanisation has reduced agriculture's share of GDP but not reduced land's role as collateral, wealth store, or political resource. Forest Rights Act (2006) recognition of Adivasi claims has been partial and contested. Digital land records (DILRMP) improve transparency but do not redistribute.

### 5. The Way Forward
Scholarship points to several incomplete agendas: redistribution of ceiling-surplus land still held by states; strict enforcement of SC/ST land alienation prevention acts; expansion of community forest rights; and integration of land rights into livelihood, credit, and housing policy.
